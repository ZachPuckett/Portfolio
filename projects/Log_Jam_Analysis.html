Remote Sensing Log Jam Analysis in the Upper Columbia Region

This project was completed for the Upper Columbia Salmon Recovery Board to support their salmon habitat restoration efforts by remotely identifying log jams that serve as vital structures for salmonid reproduction, shelter, and habitat complexity.

My research sought to quantify and map log jam distributions across over 20 river reaches in the Upper Columbia Basin using high-resolution satellite imagery, while evaluating spatial patterns in relation to human-altered landscapes such as dams and urban development. This analysis allowed for the identification of reaches in most need of restoration, based on log jam scarcity and environmental disruption.

  • Used Google Earth Pro to visually inspect reaches defined by the UC RTT Prioritization Web Map, identifying log jams based on official 2024 UC RTT definitions.
  • Captured precise coordinates and metadata (imagery date, jam dimensions, log count) for each identified log jam and entered them into a structured data entry sheet.
  • Created high-resolution satellite imagery exports of selected log jams, and used historical imagery with the time slider tool in Google Earth Pro to assess changes in log jam structures over time.
  • Generated an animated GIF using ezgif.com to visually represent long-term changes in log jam presence for a single site, demonstrating the power of remote sensing time series analysis.
  • Analyzed spatial disparities between reaches with high log jam presence (e.g., Nason Creek and Beaver Creek) and those with low or no presence (e.g., Wenatchee River and Peshastin Creek), connecting these patterns to proximity to dams (like the Dryden Dam) and urban centers (such as Leavenworth).

The final map and visual outputs revealed that log jams were concentrated in Nason Creek and Beaver Creek, while Peshastin Creek and Wenatchee River had minimal to no log jams, likely due to manmade hydrological disruptions and geomorphic alterations. These insights will inform targeted restoration priorities and emphasize the need to consider remote sensing and GIS tools for future ecological monitoring in riparian systems.
